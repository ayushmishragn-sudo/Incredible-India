History of the Golden Temple

The Golden Temple, also known as Harmandir Sahib or Darbar Sahib, is located in Amritsar, Punjab. It is the holiest shrine in Sikhism and was built in 1604 by Guru Arjan Dev, the fifth Sikh Guru. The temple houses the Adi Granth, the central religious scripture of Sikhism.

Throughout its history, the Golden Temple has undergone several renovations and restorations, most notably under Maharaja Ranjit Singh, who covered the upper floors with gold in the early 19th century, giving it its iconic appearance.

Architectural Splendor of the Golden Temple

The Golden Temple blends Hindu and Islamic architectural styles, creating a structure of unparalleled beauty and spiritual significance. Key features include:

  • The Golden Dome: The upper floors of the temple are adorned with gold plating, reflecting beautifully in the surrounding water.
  • The Sarovar (Holy Pool): A large water tank surrounding the temple, believed to have healing powers.
  • The Causeway: A marble walkway leading to the sanctum, symbolizing the journey toward enlightenment.
  • Intricate Inlay Work: The walls feature exquisite marble inlay with floral patterns and verses from the Guru Granth Sahib.

Visiting Information

The Golden Temple is open 24/7 to devotees and visitors. The best time to visit is early in the morning or late at night when the temple is beautifully illuminated.

The Langar (community kitchen) serves free meals to thousands of people every day, symbolizing equality and service. Visitors are required to cover their heads, remove their shoes, and maintain decorum while inside the temple complex.

Nearby attractions include the Jallianwala Bagh, Partition Museum, and Wagah Border ceremony.
