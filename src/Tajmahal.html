A Monument of Eternal Love

History of Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal is a white marble mausoleum located in Agra, India, built by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his beloved wife, Mumtaz Mahal. The construction began in 1632, and it was completed in 1653. The monument is a symbol of undying love and has become one of the Seven Wonders of the World.

Emperor Shah Jahan was deeply devoted to his wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who died during childbirth. In her memory, he decided to build the Taj Mahal as a tomb for her, which would reflect the splendor of their love.

Architecture of the Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal is a masterpiece of Mughal architecture, combining elements of Persian, Ottoman Turkish, and Indian architectural styles. The central structure is a massive dome made of pure white marble, and the entire complex is surrounded by lush gardens, reflecting pools, and a mosque.

Some key architectural features include:

  • The central dome, which is 35 meters high and is surrounded by four smaller domes.
  • The symmetrical design of the gardens and the reflecting pools that mirror the grandeur of the structure.
  • Intricate carvings and inlays of precious stones, including jasper, turquoise, and coral, in the marble surface.
  • The minarets, each over 40 meters tall, which are designed to lean slightly outward to avoid the risk of collapsing onto the tomb.

Visiting Information

The Taj Mahal is open every day except Fridays. The best time to visit is early in the morning or during sunset, as the light creates beautiful reflections in the marble and the surrounding pools.

Visitors can also explore the surrounding gardens, the mosque, and the tomb of Shah Jahan, who was buried beside his wife, Mumtaz Mahal.

Ticket prices vary based on nationality, with foreign tourists generally paying a higher fee. There are also separate tickets for visiting the main mausoleum, the gardens, and the mosque. Guided tours are available to help visitors learn more about the history and architecture of the Taj Mahal.
